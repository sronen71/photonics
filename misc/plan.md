## Stage 1: Understand the paper and literature

Briefly identify:
- the paper’s main contribution, assumptions, limitations, conjectures, and unresolved questions;
- the most important older papers it builds on, especially key cited papers;
- important newer papers that cite it, including work that confirms, criticizes, generalizes, applies, or supersedes it;
- relevant adjacent literature that may not cite it directly.

Use reliable sources and do not invent references. Distinguish clearly between open questions stated in the literature and gaps you infer yourself.

## Stage 2: Generate research directions

Generate candidate directions systematically from the paper’s results, assumptions, methods, limitations, and connections to other work. Consider, where relevant:

- simplifying a derivation, model, computation, or experiment;
- clarifying the physical mechanism or interpretation;
- proving or disproving a conjecture;
- strengthening a result or weakening its assumptions;
- improving precision, efficiency, robustness, or optimization;
- extending the model to new regimes, systems, interactions, dimensions, or conditions;
- connecting the result with another theory, method, or phenomenon;
- applying an established technique that has not yet been used effectively on the problem;
- deriving new predictions, experiments, numerical tests, counterexamples, or benchmarks.

Avoid vague suggestions. Each direction should be a concrete scientific question or target result.
This is a brainstorming stage: don't evaluate or prioritize yet.

## Stage 3: Evaluate and prioritize

For each strong candidate, give:

1. The research question or target result.
2. Why it matters.
3. What is already known and what appears genuinely open.
4. A plausible approach and the first decisive test.
5. Why it fits—or does not fit—my strengths and tastes.
6. Main risks and likely failure modes.
7. The value of partial or negative results.
8. A qualitative assessment of:
   - novelty;
   - importance if successful;
   - feasibility;

Conclude with a ranked shortlist of 3 directions, including:
- best overall fit;
- best near-term project;
